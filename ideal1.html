  • We strip away details that are unnecessary for solving the problem.
  • We idealize the raindrop as a small spherical object with constant mass.
  • We also assume the motion is one-dimensional (top to bottom), with no wind effects.
  • Gravity speeds the object up while air resistance slows it down. When the forces balance, the net force is zero, so by inertia it continues at a constant terminal velocity.


When you stick your hand slightly outside a car that is at rest, you don’t feel any force from the air. But when the car starts moving, you feel a push from the air in the direction opposite to the car’s motion. This push increases as the speed of the car increases.

A similar effect happens for objects falling through air. As an object falls, air exerts a drag force opposite to its motion. As the object’s speed increases, this drag force also increases.

At a certain point, the velocity of the object becomes high enough that the drag force equals its weight. At this point, the net force on the object becomes zero. From then on, the object continues to move with constant velocity due to inertia. This constant velocity is called terminal velocity.
